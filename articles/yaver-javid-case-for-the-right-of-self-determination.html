Case For Self Determination
Author : Yaver Javid
The state and government must follow ethics. In a democratic system, this must be enforced by the state upon itself and on the government. The best model I can think of includes a fixed list of rights as part of the state’s foundation; any government must operate within that framework. Even if violating one of these rights makes governance more efficient, the state must never be allowed to do so. Rights are things that man inherently deserves. At the beginning, when man finds himself insecure, he “buys” security as a service. This is a transaction. Fundamentally, whatever man owns can only be taken from him in two situations:
  1. When he willingly gives it up, either in exchange for something or as a gift.
  2. When he owes something because of consequences—for example, compensating someone for accidental harm, or returning money that he stole.
The first situation is simply a transaction. It’s consensual. Over time, large security structures establish themselves, and large numbers of men employ them for protection through these transactions. If a man wants to set up an alternate security structure, he should be free to do so. Ethically, nothing can force him to pay for a security structure he does not wish to engage with. Ethics allows for fragmented, overlapping security systems. There is nothing in ethics that grants a security structure sovereignty over men without their consent. The nature of man will almost always produce maps where these structures overlap. Even if we agree that this configuration is impractical, we can at least agree to push for smaller, practical, sovereign geographical fragments. The more fragments there are, the closer we move toward an ethical model, because man naturally paints overlaps.
